## PART 1: THE PROBLEM — OUR QUEST FOR HAPPINESS

---

### SLIDE 2: Beatitude = Supreme Happiness

*[Click to advance to Slide 2]*

Let's start with the word itself: *Beatitude*.

In Latin, the root is *beātus*—which means blessed, happy, fortunate.

But the Greek word Jesus actually used is *makários*—and this translation better captures what Jesus was really going for. *Makários* doesn't just mean "nice" or "content." It means *the happiness of the gods*. A state beyond ordinary joy.

So when Jesus says "Blessed are the poor in spirit," what he's really saying is:

*"How fortunate you are..."*  
*"How fulfilled you will be..."*  
*"How lucky you are..."*

This changes everything. Jesus isn't giving us a list of rules. He's giving us a path to the deepest happiness we can experience.

The Catechism puts it beautifully: *"The Beatitudes respond to the natural desire for happiness. This desire is of divine origin: God has placed it in the human heart in order to draw man to the One who alone can fulfill it."*

Did you catch that? The desire for happiness isn't a distraction from God—it's *designed by God* to lead us to Him.

So if beatitude means supreme happiness... why does the path to it seem so strange, so impractical?

To understand that, we need to think about what our culture has been teaching us about happiness—and how to get it.

---

### SLIDE 3: Our Quest for Happiness

*[Click to advance to Slide 3]*

**[Pause to let them see the self-help books image]**

Look at that image. How many of you have read at least one of those books?

*[Hands go up]*

Yeah, me too. *Atomic Habits. The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People. Think and Grow Rich. The Power of Now.*

The self-help industry generates $13.4 billion dollars a year. Why? Because everyone is hungry for the answer to the same question: *How do I become happy?*

Sixteen hundred years ago, St. Augustine wrote the answer: *"You have made us for yourself, O Lord, and our hearts are restless until they rest in you."*

We sense within ourselves a hunger—an infinite longing—for something more. Something that $13.4 billion worth of self-help books can't quite deliver.

Here's how Bishop Barron puts it: *"We are hungry for God—but we try to fill that hunger with something less than God."*

*[Pause and let that land]*

Let me ask you:

**What do you think are the things we reach for instead of God? What do we try to fill that hunger with?**

*[Allow 1-2 minutes for responses. Gently guide toward: money, success, comfort, pleasure, status, approval, control, achievement, relationships]*

Great answers. Thomas Aquinas, 800 years ago, identified exactly four categories.

---

### SLIDE 4: The Four Substitutes

*[Click to advance to Slide 4]*

First: **WEALTH.** *"If I just had enough..."*

Not just money. It's the belief that material security will satisfy us. If I could just afford that house, that car, that retirement fund—*then* I'd be happy.

Second: **PLEASURE.** *"If I just felt good enough..."*

Good feelings. Pleasant sensations—physical, emotional, psychological. The belief that if I could just *feel* good all the time, I'd be content.

Third: **POWER.** *"If I could just control enough..."*

The need to be in charge. To control outcomes, situations, people. If I could just get everyone to do what I want, *then* things would be okay.

Fourth: **HONOR.** *"If enough people respected me..."*

Approval. Recognition. Reputation. If people would just see me the way I want to be seen—*then* I'd feel complete.

*[Pause]*

Now, here's the thing. None of these are *evil* in themselves. Money isn't bad. Pleasure isn't bad. Influence isn't bad. Being respected isn't bad.

The problem comes when we put them at the *center* of our concern. When we make them into *gods*. When we believe—consciously or not—that *this* is where happiness lives.

---

### SLIDE 5: When We Shift the Center

*[Click to advance to Slide 5]*

Now, some of you might remember from last week's session on Saints and Holiness—Jaisy used the image of a bicycle wheel to describe the path to holiness.

*[Point to the healthy wheel on the left]*

When God is at the center—the hub—everything else radiates outward in proper order. Family, work, health, finances, rest, faith... all the things of our lives become spokes that connect to God at the center. The wheel is balanced. It rolls smoothly. Everything works.

*[Point to the broken wheel on the right]*

But watch what happens when we take God out of the center and put something else there.

Here I've put Wealth at the center—but it could be any of the four substitutes. Notice what happens to the wheel:

The rim becomes distorted. Some spokes are bent. Others are broken. The whole structure is off-balance.

Why? Because a finite thing cannot hold the center. It wasn't designed to. When we put something less than God at the center of our lives, *everything else gets pulled out of alignment*.

*[Point to the four substitute buttons below]*

And remember—all those things on the rim? Family, work, health, honor, pleasure? Every single one of them can be sorted into these four categories. Wealth. Pleasure. Power. Honor.

When any of these becomes the center instead of God, the wheel can't roll straight anymore.

And when something finite stays at the center for too long... there's a word for that. We call it *addiction*.

---

### SLIDE 6: The Cycle of Addiction

*[Click to advance to Slide 6]*

Let me show you how it works.

It starts here: We are *hungry for God*. That infinite longing Augustine talked about.

But instead of turning to God, we *reach for something less*. One of those four substitutes.

And what happens? We're *frustrated*. Because finite things can never satisfy an infinite longing.

So what do we do? We convince ourselves we *need more*. More money. More pleasure. More control. More approval.

And eventually... we become *enslaved*. We're obsessively turning around something that can never, in principle, make us happy.

*[Pause and let them see the cycle]*

Look at the center: NEVER SATISFIED. That's where we end up. Spinning in circles. Going nowhere. Getting more and more exhausted.

Look at the modern manifestations: Workaholism. Status seeking. Substances. Achievement. Approval. Control.

*[Optional personal moment - keep it brief]*

I'll be honest with you. I know this cycle intimately. In my own life, I've chased achievement like it was going to save me. I've needed people's approval so badly that I would spiral for days when I didn't get it. The same drive that made me successful in my career is the same drive that can keep me spiritually sick.

And that's the trap, isn't it? These substitutes *work*—at least for a while. Until they don't.

And that answer to our deepest longing isn't more self-help. It's not "try harder" or "want less."

Jesus gave us the answer on a mountainside 2,000 years ago in what people typically call the "greatest sermon ever preached."

### SLIDE 8: Detachment and Action

*[Click to advance to Slide 8]*

Here's the key to understanding all eight Beatitudes.

*[Point to the left card]*

Bishop Barron teaches that the Beatitudes fall into two movements. The first movement is **Detachment**—*Letting Go*.

Four of the Beatitudes are about releasing our grip on those four substitutes we talked about. Freedom *from* addiction.

*[Let them take in the hands image—silver hand releasing, golden hand receiving, cross in center]*

*[Point to the right card]*

The second movement is **Action**—*Moving Toward*.

The other four Beatitudes are about what we're freed *for*. When we let go of what enslaves us, we become free to love. Freedom *for* love.

*[Point to the bottom tagline]*

*"First we let go... then we are free to love."*

That's the whole framework. Two movements. Eight Beatitudes. One path to happiness.

Now let's look at each one. First, the four Detachment Beatitudes—what we need to let go of.

## PART 3A: THE DETACHMENT BEATITUDES

---

### SLIDE 9: The Detachment Beatitudes (Section Title)

*[Click to advance to Slide 9]*

We'll start with the four Detachment Beatitudes. 

I'll go through each one relatively quickly. For each, I'll show you the actual verse, then a modern translation—what Jesus is really saying—and finally, what it frees you from, with real-world examples we can all relate to.

Let's begin.

---

### SLIDE 10: Poor in Spirit

*[Click to advance to Slide 10]*

*[Build 1: Title and verse appear]*

*"Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven."*

*[Build 2: What Jesus is Really Saying appears]*

What Jesus is really saying: **How lucky you are if you're not attached to material things.**

When the kingdom of God is your ultimate concern—not your bank account, not your possessions—you won't become addicted to material things. And paradoxically, you'll be able to use them with great effectiveness for God's purposes.

*[Build 3: Frees You From circle + examples appear]*

This beatitude frees us from the addiction to **Wealth**.

We see this in materialism—always needing the next thing. Financial anxiety—that knot in your stomach when you check your accounts. And that "never enough" feeling—no matter how much you have, it's never quite enough.

---

### SLIDE 11: Those Who Mourn

*[Click to advance to Slide 11]*

*[Build 1: Title and verse appear]*

*"Blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted."*

*[Build 2: What Jesus is Really Saying appears]*

What Jesus is really saying: **How lucky you are if you're not addicted to good feelings.**

Now this one can sound like masochism. But here's the point: Good feelings—physical, emotional, psychological—are wonderful. But they're not God. And if we turn them into God, they become an addiction. This isn't Puritanism. It's about detachment and spiritual freedom.

*[Build 3: Frees You From circle + examples appear]*

This beatitude frees us from the addiction to **Pleasure**.

We see this in substances—anything we use to change how we feel. Numbing out—binge-watching, scrolling, eating, shopping—anything to avoid discomfort. And comfort obsession—when avoiding pain becomes more important than pursuing purpose.

---

### SLIDE 12: The Meek

*[Click to advance to Slide 12]*

*[Build 1: Title and verse appear]*

*"Blessed are the meek, for they shall inherit the earth."*

*[Build 2: What Jesus is Really Saying appears]*

What Jesus is really saying: **How lucky you are if you're not attached to worldly power.**

Jesus isn't passing judgment on institutions of power. He's showing a path of detachment. When you're detached from worldly power, you can follow God's will—even if it means walking a path of extreme powerlessness.

*[Build 3: Frees You From circle + examples appear]*

This beatitude frees us from the addiction to **Power**.

We see this in control—needing everything to go our way. Domination—having to win every argument, every situation. And manipulation—pulling strings to get the outcomes we want.

---

### SLIDE 13: Persecuted

*[Click to advance to Slide 13]*

*[Build 1: Title and verse appear]*

*"Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness' sake, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven."*

*[Build 2: What Jesus is Really Saying appears]*

What Jesus is really saying: **How lucky you are if you're not addicted to others' approval.**

Think about how much mental energy we spend worrying about what others think of us. How many of us have woken up at 3 AM rehearsing conversations, seeking approval we didn't get?

Jesus says: you're lucky if you can let that go.

*[Build 3: Frees You From circle + examples appear]*

This beatitude frees us from the addiction to **Honor**.

We see this in people-pleasing—saying yes when we mean no, just to be liked. Status seeking—needing the right title, the right neighborhood, the right clothes. And fear of criticism—letting what others *might* think stop us from doing what's right.

*[Pause]*

So those are the four Detachment Beatitudes—what we need to let go of. Now let's look at the four Action Beatitudes—what we become free *for*.

## PART 3B: THE ACTION BEATITUDES

---

### SLIDE 14: The Action Beatitudes (Section Title)

*[Click to advance to Slide 14]*

Now for the four Action Beatitudes.

The Detachment Beatitudes freed us *from* addiction — those four substitutes that enslave us.

The Action Beatitudes free us *for* something — they point us toward **virtue**.

What's a virtue? The Catechism says:

> *"A virtue is a habitual and firm disposition to do the good. It allows the person not only to perform good acts, but to give the best of himself... The goal of a virtuous life is to become like God."*
> — CCC, 1803

It's not just doing a good thing once — it's becoming the kind of person who *naturally* does good things. It's who you *are*, not just what you *do*.

Each of these four beatitudes cultivates a specific virtue:
- **Purpose** — knowing what you're living for
- **Compassion** — genuine care for others
- **Clarity** — an undivided heart that sees clearly
- **Harmony** — bringing peace wherever you go

Same format as before: the verse, what Jesus is really saying, and what it frees you for — with real-world examples.

Let's continue.

---

### SLIDE 15: Hunger for Righteousness

*[Click to advance to Slide 15]*

*[On load: Title and verse appear]*

*"Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they shall be satisfied."*

*[Build 1: What Jesus is Really Saying appears]*

What Jesus is really saying: When you wake up, your first thought is *"What's the right thing to do today?"* — not "What will get me ahead?"

Think about that. Most of us wake up thinking about our to-do list, our problems, what we need to accomplish. But what if the first question was simply: *What's the right thing?*

That's a completely different orientation to life.

*[Build 2: Frees You For appears]*

This beatitude frees you for **Purpose**.

When doing the right thing becomes your deepest hunger, you gain direction. You have integrity — your actions match your values. You have moral clarity — you know what matters and what doesn't.

You're not drifting anymore. You have a compass.

---

### SLIDE 16: The Merciful

*[Click to advance to Slide 16]*

*[On load: Title and verse appear]*

*"Blessed are the merciful, for they shall obtain mercy."*

*[Build 1: What Jesus is Really Saying appears]*

What Jesus is really saying: You stop keeping score. You give people *what they need*, not what they deserve.

We all keep mental scorecards, don't we? Who owes us. Who wronged us. Who hasn't apologized yet.

The merciful person tears up the scorecard. They give grace — not because the other person earned it, but because that's who they've become.

*[Build 2: Frees You For appears]*

This beatitude frees you for **Compassion**.

When you stop keeping score, something opens up in you. You become capable of real forgiveness — not just saying the words, but actually letting go. You develop tenderness toward people who are struggling. You find patience with people who would have driven you crazy before.

You become a channel of God's mercy to the world.

---

### SLIDE 17: Pure of Heart

*[Click to advance to Slide 17]*

*[On load: Title and verse appear]*

*"Blessed are the pure of heart, for they shall see God."*

*[Build 1: What Jesus is Really Saying appears]*

What Jesus is really saying: Your heart isn't divided. You're not trying to serve two masters — *God is the one thing* at the center.

Remember that bicycle wheel from earlier? This is what it looks like when God is truly at the center. Not competing with career. Not competing with approval. Not competing with comfort.

One thing. Undivided.

*[Build 2: Frees You For appears]*

This beatitude frees you for **Clarity**.

When your heart is undivided, you can finally see clearly. You gain focus — you know what to pay attention to and what to ignore. You gain vision — you can see God at work in your life and in others. And you experience undivided love — you're not pulled in a thousand directions anymore.

The promise is "they shall see God." When your heart is pure, you start seeing Him everywhere.

---

### SLIDE 18: Peacemakers

*[Click to advance to Slide 18]*

*[On load: Title and verse appear]*

*"Blessed are the peacemakers, for they shall be called children of God."*

*[Build 1: What Jesus is Really Saying appears]*

What Jesus is really saying: You become *the calm in the storm*. People around you become better just by being near you.

This is the fruit of everything else. When you've let go of the four addictions, when you hunger for righteousness, show mercy, and have a pure heart — you radiate something. People feel it.

You walk into a tense room and somehow the temperature drops. You're the person people call when things fall apart.

*[Build 2: Frees You For appears]*

This beatitude frees you for **Harmony**.

You become someone who reconciles — who brings divided people back together. You have a calm presence — you don't add fuel to fires. And you're a bridge-builder — you can connect people who would never connect on their own.

That's what it means to be called a child of God. You start doing what God does — bringing peace where there was chaos.

*[Pause]*

---

*[Transition to next section]*

Okay. So now you have the full framework. 

Four Detachment Beatitudes — freedom FROM addiction to wealth, pleasure, power, and honor.

Four Action Beatitudes — freedom FOR purpose, compassion, clarity, and harmony.

But what does this actually *look like* when someone lives it out?

I want to explore that with you. And I want to start by hearing YOUR answers.

### SLIDE 22: What Guido Showed Us

*[Click to advance to Slide 22]*

Let me walk you through what we just saw—both movements of the heart.

*[Point to the left column - Detachment]*

**What Guido let go of:**

- **Wealth:** He had *nothing*—stripped of all possessions. Yet he created richness from imagination alone.
- **Pleasure:** He faced terror every moment—yet he set aside his own fear and grief to bring joy to his son.

- **Mercy:** He had no hope to give. He gave it anyway. "Good morning, Princess!" to his wife—hope from nothing.
- **Purity:** Everything flowed from one undivided love—his son. One purpose. Pure of heart.
- **Peace:** He created pockets of peace in the horror of the concentration camp. Music through the window. Moments of grace.

## PART 6: THE PERFECT EXAMPLE — CHRIST CRUCIFIED

---

### SLIDE 23: The Perfect Example

*[Click to advance to Slide 23]*

Here's a question:

**What's a *perfect* example of the Beatitudes?**

Thomas Aquinas gave us the answer. He said: If you want the perfect exemplification of the Beatitudes, look to **Christ Crucified**.

---

### SLIDE 24: Christ Crucified

*[Click to advance to Slide 24]*

**[Let them take in the image of the Isenheim Altarpiece]**

This is Matthias Grünewald's Isenheim Altarpiece—one of the most spiritually powerful and brutally realistic depictions of the Crucifixion in Western art.

Look at Jesus. His mouth agape in speechless anguish. The terrible wounds in his hands and feet. The blisters all over his body. There's nothing sentimental about this.

Thomas Aquinas said: *"If you want to be happy—despise what Jesus despised on the cross, and love what Jesus loved on the cross."*

*[Point to "What He Despised" section]*

What did Jesus *despise* on the cross? Those four substitutes.

- **Wealth** — He had none. He was stripped naked.
- **Pleasure** — He was at the limit of physical and psychological suffering.
- **Power** — He had none. He was nailed to the cross. He couldn't even move.
- **Honor** — They mocked him publicly as he died.

Total detachment.

*[Point to "What He Loved" section]*

What did Jesus *love* on the cross?

- **Righteousness** — Doing the Father's will.
- **Mercy** — The ultimate bearer of divine mercy.
- **Purity** — The single-hearted one.
- **Peace** — The ultimate peacemaker.

Pure action.

*[Pause]*

Here's the paradox. Bishop Barron says: "Though it's very strange to say, if Aquinas is right, *that* is a picture of a happy man."

It sounds insane. But all of our expectations have to be turned around. And then we can see: *that* is a picture of freedom. *That* is a picture of joy.

---

## CLOSING

*[Pause. Let the image remain on screen.]*

The formula is simple:

**Detachment + Action = Beatitude.**

First we let go... then we are free to love.

Remember that bicycle wheel. When God is at the center, everything else finds its proper place. When something else is at the center, everything falls apart.

The Beatitudes are Jesus's prescription for putting God back at the center—and keeping Him there.

*[Pause]*

Let me close with something Bishop Barron said that has stayed with me:

*"Christian ethics doesn't begin with the law. It doesn't begin with 'no.' It begins with happiness. What makes you happy? That's what God wants. The rules, the prohibitions—they're not to shame you. They're to bring you to the path that leads to beatitude."*

The Beatitudes aren't impossible ideals. They're a prescription. Medicine for what ails us.

May we all have the courage to let go of what enslaves us—and the love to give what truly matters.

In the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit. Amen.
